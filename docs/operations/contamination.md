# Contamination Reporting

Contamination is a serious risk in mushroom cultivation. MycoERP provides a structured system for reporting, tracking, and resolving contamination incidents.

## When to File a Report

File a contamination report whenever you observe:

- Visible mold growth that is not the intended mycelium (green, black, orange, pink, or yellow patches).
- Unusual odors from a batch (sour, sweet, or ammonia-like smells).
- Wet or slimy spots that suggest bacterial contamination.
- Abnormal growth patterns (stunted mycelium, unusual textures).
- Fruit body abnormalities that suggest infection.

When in doubt, file a report. It is better to have a false alarm investigated than to let contamination spread undetected.

## Creating a Contamination Report

1. Navigate to "Contamination" in the sidebar.
2. Click "New Report".
3. Fill in the required fields:
   - **Batch** -- Select the affected batch from the dropdown.
   - **Observed At** -- When you first noticed the issue (defaults to now).
   - **Severity** -- How bad the contamination appears (see severity levels below).
   - **Affected Quantity** -- How many units in the batch appear affected (e.g., 3 out of 12 bags).
4. Fill in the optional but recommended fields:
   - **Suspected Type** -- What kind of contamination you think it is (e.g., Trichoderma, Cobweb mold, Bacteria, Unknown).
   - **Suspected Cause** -- Your best guess at how it happened (e.g., "Possible filter failure during inoculation", "Bag developed a small tear").
   - **Action Taken** -- What you did immediately (e.g., "Isolated affected bags from the rest", "Moved to quarantine area").
   - **Photo** -- Upload a photo showing the contamination. This helps managers assess severity remotely.
5. Click "Submit Report".

## Severity Levels

- **Low** -- Minor contamination confined to a small area. Batch may still be salvageable if caught early. Example: A single small green spot on one agar plate out of twenty.

- **Medium** -- Moderate contamination affecting a noticeable portion of the batch. Requires investigation and likely removal of affected units. Example: Three out of twelve grain spawn jars show signs of bacterial growth.

- **High** -- Significant contamination affecting a large portion of the batch or suggesting a systemic issue. Requires immediate management attention. Example: Half of the substrate bags in a rack are showing Trichoderma.

- **Critical** -- Severe contamination that threatens other batches or indicates a major breach in sterile procedure. Requires immediate isolation and root cause investigation. Example: All recently inoculated bags across multiple racks are contaminated, suggesting a contaminated spawn source.

## What Happens After You File a Report

1. The report is saved with status "Open".
2. A notification is sent to farm managers and administrators.
3. The batch's health status may be updated to reflect the contamination.
4. A manager will review the report and add their assessment.

## Report Statuses

- **Open** -- Just filed, awaiting manager review.
- **Reviewed** -- A manager has seen the report and added their notes or instructions.
- **Resolved** -- The issue has been handled (contaminated units removed, procedures corrected, etc.).
- **Closed** -- The incident is fully documented and no further action needed.

## Manager Review Process

When a manager reviews a contamination report:

1. They examine the photos and worker's notes.
2. They visit the affected area if needed.
3. They add a "Manager Note" to the report with their assessment and instructions.
4. They change the status to "Reviewed".
5. They may update the batch health status (e.g., change from "Healthy" to "Partially Contaminated" or "Contaminated").
6. They decide on corrective actions (isolate, discard, investigate further).
7. Once actions are complete, they mark the report as "Resolved" and eventually "Closed".

## Impact on Batches

Filing a contamination report does not automatically change the batch status. Managers decide whether to:

- Mark the batch as "Partially Contaminated" (some salvageable).
- Mark the batch as "Contaminated" (all affected).
- Mark the batch as "Discarded" (removed from production).
- Keep the batch status unchanged if the contamination was minor and contained.

## Best Practices

- Report contamination as soon as you see it. Early detection limits spread.
- Always isolate suspected contaminated items immediately -- move them away from healthy batches.
- Take photos before disturbing the affected items.
- Note everything: when you first noticed it, what it looks like, what it smells like.
- If you see contamination in multiple batches of the same type, mention this in the report -- it may indicate a systemic issue (contaminated spawn source, failed sterilization cycle, etc.).
- Check neighboring batches when contamination is found. It may have already spread.
- Wash hands and change gloves after handling contaminated items before touching other batches.
